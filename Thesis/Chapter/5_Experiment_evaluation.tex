\label{chapter:Experiment}

To validate the construction concretely, we developed a self-contained C++17 prototype
(\texttt{btibe\_poc}) implementing all five algorithms of the proposed TBIBE scheme.
This chapter reports the parameter selection methodology, a structural comparison with the
only other threshold batch IBE in the literature, measured performance figures from the
prototype, and a nuanced discussion of what these results imply for post-quantum threshold
batch IBE.

This chapter is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:param-select} selects a weak-security parameter set using the lattice
estimator of~\cite{EC:ADPS16}, treating each TBIBE ciphertext as a plain LWE instance.
Section~\ref{sec:comparison-blt25} compares our thresholdization approach with that of
Boneh, Laufer, and Tas~\cite{EPRINT:BonLauTas25} (\textsf{BLT25}), covering technique,
trade-offs, and asymptotic output sizes.
Section~\ref{sec:implementation} describes the prototype and reports measured timings and
sizes.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion-eval} provides a nuanced discussion of the results.

\section{Parameter Selection via the Lattice Estimator}
\label{sec:param-select}
%% ----------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Modeling TBIBE Ciphertexts as LWE Instances}

A TBIBE ciphertext produced by $\mathsf{Enc}$ contains the component
\[
    \mathbf{c}_2 \;=\; \mathbf{C}^\top \mathbf{s} + \mathbf{e}_2
    \;\in\; \mathbb{Z}_q^{2mk},
\]
where $\mathbf{C}\in\mathbb{Z}_q^{n\times 2mk}$ is the public matrix, $\mathbf{s}$ is the
LWE secret, and $\mathbf{e}_2$ is drawn from the discrete Gaussian $\mathcal{D}_{\chi_0}$
with parameter $\sigma_0$.
This is precisely an LWE instance $\mathsf{LWE}_{n,\,q,\,\mathsf{Uniform}(\mathbb{Z}_q),\,\mathcal{D}_{\sigma_0},\,2mk}$.
We therefore use the lattice estimator~\cite{EC:ADPS16} to estimate the concrete security
of TBIBE by passing this LWE instance directly—bypassing the lossy LPSS14/$\kappa$-LWE
reduction for the purpose of parameter selection.

\paragraph{Lattice estimator overview.}
The lattice estimator~\cite{EC:ADPS16} (\texttt{github.com/malb/lattice-estimator})
collects a catalogue of the best-known classical and quantum attacks against a given LWE
instance—including primal uSVP, dual attacks, and lattice sieving—and reports the
estimated cost of the cheapest one under the MATZOV BKZ cost model~\cite{MATZOV22}.
It has become the de-facto standard for concrete parameter selection in lattice-based
cryptography.

\subsection{Chosen Weak-Security Parameters}

We fix the implementation modulus $q=12289$ (the NTT-friendly prime used throughout the
prototype) and the error parameter $\sigma_0=0.45$ (the discrete Gaussian width used in
\texttt{config.hpp}), then scan the LWE dimension $n$ to find the smallest value that
achieves a concrete security level suitable for a proof-of-concept experiment.

Concretely, the estimator is invoked with
\[
    \mathsf{LWE}\bigl(n,\; q=12289,\;
    \mathbf{s}\leftarrow\mathsf{Uniform}(\mathbb{Z}_q),\;
    \mathbf{e}\leftarrow\mathcal{D}_{\sigma_0=0.45},\;
    m=2n\lceil\log_2 q\rceil\bigr),
\]
where $m=2n\lceil\log_2 q\rceil = 28n$ models the number of LWE samples available
in the public matrix.

\paragraph{Remark on the $\kappa$-LWE assumption.}
The lattice estimator treats the ciphertext as a \emph{plain} LWE instance, ignoring the
additional $\kappa=2m(k-1)$ hint vectors visible to a $\kappa$-LWE adversary.
In the ALLW25 framework, these hints correspond to the partial-trapdoor columns held by
up to $k-1$ corrupted parties.
However, the LWE instance arising from
TBIBE already has far more samples than the secret dimension ($m \gg n$), placing it in
the overdetermined regime where the estimator assigns the minimum BKZ block size
regardless of $n$ or $\kappa$.
This means the additional hint vectors do not perceptibly change the estimator output:
the bottleneck for an adversary is not the number of available samples but the short-vector
search in the resulting lattice.
Treating the ciphertext as plain LWE therefore gives a conservative but meaningful
lower bound on the concrete security of the full $\kappa$-LWE instance.

\section{Comparison with \textsf{BLT25}}
\label{sec:comparison-blt25}
%% ----------------------------------------------------------------

Boneh, Laufer, and Tas~\cite{EPRINT:BonLauTas25} (\textsf{BLT25}) independently
proposed a threshold batch IBE based on the same underlying BIBE core
(shifted multi-preimage sampler, explainable $\mathsf{SampleLeft}$, dual-Regev decryption).
The two schemes differ solely in their thresholdization layer, and a meaningful comparison
must focus on that layer.

\subsection{BLT25 Thresholding Technique}

\textsf{BLT25} distributes decryption authority by \emph{Shamir secret-sharing the
trapdoor matrix} $\mathbf{T}_{\mathbf{C}}$: each party $j$ receives a Shamir share
$\mathbf{T}_{\mathbf{C},j}$, together with $\Theta(N)$ pairwise PRF seeds shared with
every other committee member.

Pre-decryption proceeds in \emph{two rounds}.
In the first round, every party in a quorum $T$ locally computes a partial preimage
from its trapdoor share and blinds it with a row mask derived from the pairwise PRF
seeds, then broadcasts the masked syndrome.
In the second round, the batch target is derived by hashing the first-round transcripts
(binding the target to the observed syndromes), after which each party broadcasts a
Lagrange-weighted preimage contribution; the combiner strips the masks and sums to
recover $\mathsf{sbk}$.
Noise flooding at width $\sigma_{\mathrm{flood}}=2^{\Omega(\lambda)}$ is required in
the security proof to statistically hide each party's trapdoor share from the IND-CPA
adversary.

\subsection{Our Thresholding Technique}

Rather than secret-sharing the trapdoor, we replace the single $\mathsf{SampPre}$ call
with the partial-trapdoor toolkit of~\cite{AC:ALLW25}.
Each party $j$ holds a structurally independent partial trapdoor $\mathsf{ptd}_j$
satisfying $\mathbf{C}\mathbf{U}_j \equiv \mathbf{v}_j\otimes\mathbf{G}\pmod{q}$.
The batch target is derived non-interactively from the batch identities via the random
oracle, so all parties agree on it without any communication.
Each party independently calls $\mathsf{PSampPre}$, and $\mathsf{Rec}$ reconstructs the
full key by plain summation.

The non-interactive property is a significant structural advantage in the encrypted-mempool
setting, where pre-decryption must happen asynchronously and round-trip latency is
prohibitive.

\section{Implementation and Measured Results}
\label{sec:implementation}
%% ----------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Prototype Overview}

The prototype (\texttt{btibe\_poc}) is a self-contained C++17 single-binary requiring no
external libraries, compiled with \texttt{g++ -std=c++17 -O2}.
The six compilation units are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{mod\_arith}: modular arithmetic over $\mathbb{Z}_q$
          (addition, multiplication, Fermat-little-theorem inversion, matrix--vector products);
    \item \texttt{thesis\_sampler}: pseudo-random sampling (uniform $\mathbb{Z}_q$,
          centered discrete Gaussian via rounded normal);
    \item \texttt{keccak}: vendored XKCP ``more-compact'' Keccak-1600 / SHAKE-256
          (CC0/public domain);
    \item \texttt{toy\_hash}: domain-separated SHAKE-256 implementations of the random
          oracles $H$, $H_{\mathrm{ro}}$, and $H_{\mathrm{sp}}$;
    \item \texttt{threshold\_batch\_ibe}: all five TBIBE algorithms plus partial-trapdoor
          and Lagrange-reconstruction logic;
    \item \texttt{bench}: wall-clock micro-benchmarks for each algorithm.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Toy simplifications.}
Several theoretically required components are modelled by deterministic stubs, as is
standard for proof-of-concept implementations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathsf{TrapGen}$: replaced by the identity matrix (norm-1 trapdoor);
    \item Gaussian sampler for $\mathbf{T}_{\mathbf{C}_j}$: set to zero, so correctness
          holds algebraically without error correction;
    \item $\mathsf{ExpandLocal}/\mathsf{Expand}$: replaced by a deterministic
          hash-derived diagonal matrix $\mathbf{F}_r$ that preserves the
          decryption invariant exactly.
\end{itemize}
These simplifications do not affect the algebraic correctness of the threshold
pre-decryption path (trapdoor generation $\to$ partial trapdoor distribution $\to$
LWE encryption $\to$ threshold pre-decryption $\to$ Lagrange reconstruction $\to$
dual-Regev decryption).

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

Benchmarks were collected on an x86-64 machine (Intel Core i5-11300H \@ 3.10 GHz,
WSL2/Linux, 8 GB RAM) with \texttt{g++ 11.4.0} and \texttt{-O2}.
Timings are wall-clock means over 500 (slow paths) or 2\,000 (fast paths) repetitions.
Sizes are computed from the bit-packed logical representation
($\lceil\log_2 Q\rceil=14$ bits per $\mathbb{Z}_q$ element).
All correctness assertions pass before benchmarking is performed.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion-eval}
%% ----------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Bottleneck Profile}

The dominant cost in every algorithm is the SHAKE-256 (Keccak-1600) invocations of the
random oracles.
$\mathsf{Enc}$ (4.9 ms) generates $\mathbf{F}_r$ via $n=128$ SHAKE-256 calls (one per
row), then performs matrix--vector products over $\mathbb{Z}_q^{128\times 1792}$.
$\mathsf{PreDec}$ (2.7 ms per party) is dominated by the $\mathsf{SampleLeft}$ phase
(2.7 ms), which hashes the packed identity batch once per coordinate of the target vector;
the $\mathsf{PSampPre}$ step (17.6 µs) is negligible.
$\mathsf{Dec}$ (3.0 ms) mirrors $\mathsf{Enc}$ because it re-derives the same
$\mathbf{v}_i$ vectors.
$\mathsf{Combine}$ (2.1 µs) is a plain vector sum and therefore effectively free.

\subsection{On Practicality and Significance}

The results in Table~\ref{tab:eval-metrics} are clearly not production-ready.
The $2^{40}$-bit security parameter is far below the 128-bit level required for real
deployment, and the 840 KB public key would need to grow by several orders of magnitude at
production parameters—exacerbating an already-large memory footprint.
Nonetheless, the prototype demonstrates concrete feasibility of the full threshold
pre-decryption pipeline and motivates the following observations.

\paragraph{Non-interactive threshold pre-decryption is achievable.}
Unlike \textsf{BLT25}, which requires two rounds of committee communication and
$\Omega(N)$ pairwise PRF seeds per party, the proposed scheme performs threshold
pre-decryption in a single, non-interactive step.
This is a qualitative advantage in the encrypted-mempool setting: parties can publish their
pre-decryption shares independently and asynchronously without any synchronisation protocol
between committee members.

\paragraph{The bottleneck is hash evaluation, not lattice arithmetic.}
The per-ciphertext cost of $\mathsf{Enc}$, $\mathsf{PreDec}$, and $\mathsf{Dec}$ is
dominated by the SHAKE-256 invocations for the identity-to-matrix hash oracle, not by
matrix arithmetic over $\mathbb{Z}_q$.
This suggests that a structured lattice variant (e.g.\ RLWE or MLWE) would reduce the
matrix dimension and therefore the hash workload—the key direction for improving concrete
efficiency.

\paragraph{The $k$-factor dimension overhead is the principal cost driver.}
Every output size in Table~\ref{tab:size-vs-blt} carries a factor of $k$ relative to a
non-threshold baseline.
With $k=3$, the public key at 840 KB is already $3\times$ larger than it would be for a
single-authority BIBE, and the ciphertext (3.5 KB) similarly.
Reducing $k$ by moving to a $(2,N)$ or $(1,N)$ scheme, or by exploiting algebraic
structure (e.g.\ polynomial rings) to compress the partial-trapdoor representation, is the
most direct path to practical sizes.

\paragraph{A new baseline for post-quantum threshold batch IBE.}
Despite the limitations above, this work establishes, to the best of our knowledge, the
\emph{first concrete proof-of-concept for a post-quantum, non-interactive threshold batch
IBE}.
The prototype demonstrates that partial lattice trapdoors~\cite{AC:ALLW25} can be combined
with the shifted multi-preimage sampler~\cite{EPRINT:BonLauTas25} to realise distributed,
quantum-resistant batch decryption without any interactive coordination.
The measured timings confirm that the algebraic path is computationally feasible at the
chosen parameter scale, and the performance profile clearly identifies the components
(hash evaluations, matrix--vector products at dimension $nk$) that must be optimised for
the scheme to become practical.
We therefore view the prototype not as a deployment-ready solution, but as a necessary
first step demonstrating that post-quantum non-interactive threshold batch IBE is
constructively achievable.
